Sierpinski's Carpet works with the idea of taking a big shape, removing a smaller shape from it using a cookie cutter, rinse and repeat. It starts by dividing up a large rectangle that covers the entire drawing area into nine smaller rectangular areas arranged in a 3×3 grid. The middle rectangle is painted in a colour (black by default). Then every one of the other eight smaller rectangles get painted in a second colour (white by default) and then the entire process is repeated inside each of the eight smaller rectangles, treating each one like the large rectangle we started with. You can see each step of the process by locating the depth control on the Geometry tab of the parameters window and reducing the depth down to one. The result is a single black rectangle. When you increase the depth to two then eight smaller black rectangles appear.

The process so far is:

  1. Paint the middle portion black.
  2. Paint each of the remaining eight portions white.
  3. Paint a small black rectangle inside the middle portion of each of those eight portions.

So far we have:

  • One background colour (white).
  • One filling colour (black).
  • No opacity (translucency). No whiteness shows through the black.
  • No blending effects like paint programs like Photoshop have.
  • The centre "filling" is just a black rectangle. We could draw something more elaborate instead.
  • The nine sections are all the same size.
  • There are nine sections, as opposed to seven or five.

All of these things can be changed however.

The centre of the image is decorated or distinguished in some way when this box is ticked and is plainer or less decorated when the box is not ticked. The precise manner in which it's decorated depends on how some other options are set. The primary filling may be applied to the corners, mid-sections or both.

Selecting this option will apply the chosen pattern to the four corner locations out of the nine total locations which exist by default at level 1 (and 4×8 corner locations out of 9×8 total locations at level 2, etc.). The four locations positioned halfway between two corners will be drawn with a simple block filling.

The four corner locations will be drawn with a simple block filling.
When this option is selected the chosen pattern will be applied as the filling in every possible location. From the nine locations that exist by default in each grid, no location will consistently have a simple rectangular block filling. However, the filling for the centre of the whole image (level 0) can be controlled separately using the "Patterned centre" tick box. The pattern depth control also has an impact.

Here you can choose colours for the nine sections of the 3×3 grid. These are applied recursively. This controls how many levels of detail are drawn opaquely without any translucency effect applied to the filling. The opacity slider for the central portion will be ignored initially and translucency will only be applied at the finer levels of detail. This option will have no effect if the centre opacity slider is positioned fully to the right, which is the default position.
This controls how many times rectangles are painted with colour. No background painting is performed at level 1. When set to 2 then the 8 large outer rectangles (default number) have their backgrounds painted once but when the finer details are added only their centre rectangles are painted. When set to 3 then 8 large rectangles are painted followed by 8×8 smaller rectangles, and everywhere except the very centre ends up being painted twice. You can build up layered effects using opacities and blend modes.
This selects whether or not the blend mode chosen above is applied to the filling (centre rectangle) in addition to being applied to the background areas. If this box is unticked then the filling will be painted in source over mode regardless of which blend mode is selected above. Most blend modes look even better when they're applied to everything (this box ticked) but sometimes a particular blend mode can badly distort the filling, in which case unticking this box improves the image. Alternatively, just experiment and use it as an extra option to play with.
This is number of levels of detail to draw. At level 1 only a single rectangle is drawn. At level 2 the centre rectangle is surrounded by 4–8 rectangles that are each one size smaller. At level 3 each those 4–8 rectangles is surrounded by 4–8 rectangles that are smaller still, and so on.
