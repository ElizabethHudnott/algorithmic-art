Sierpinski's Carpet works with the idea of taking a big shape, removing a smaller shape from it using a cookie cutter, rinse and repeat. It starts by dividing up a large rectangle that covers the entire drawing area into nine smaller rectangular areas arranged in a 3×3 grid. The middle rectangle is painted in a colour (black by default). Then every one of the other eight smaller rectangles get painted in a second colour (white by default) and then the entire process is repeated inside each of the eight smaller rectangles, treating each one like the large rectangle we started with. You can see each step of the process by locating the depth control on the Geometry tab in the parameters window and reducing the depth down to one. The result is a single black rectangle. When you increase the depth to two then eight smaller black rectangles appear.

The process so far is:

  1. Paint the middle portion black.
  2. Paint each of the remaining eight portions white.
  3. Paint a small black rectangle inside the middle portion of each of those eight portions.

So far we have:

  • One background colour (white).
  • One filling colour (black).
  • No opacity (translucency). No whiteness shows through the black.
  • No blending effects like paint programs like Photoshop have.
  • The centre "filling" is just a black rectangle. We could draw something more elaborate instead.
  • The nine sections are all the same size.

All of these things can be changed however.

This option paints a plain rectangle (black by default) in the spaces marked out by the virtual cookie cutter.
This option paints copies of an image of your choice in the spaces marked out by the virtual cookie cutter. It's an alternative option instead of having black rectangles.
This option paints concentric bands of colour in the spaces marked out by the virtual cookie cutter. It's an alternative option instead of having black rectangles.

The centre of the image is decorated or distinguished in some way when this box is ticked and is plainer or less decorated when the box is not ticked. The precise manner in which it's decorated depends on how some other options are set. It depends on whether the primary filling is applied to the corners, mid-sections or both and whether or not bipartite colouring mode is enabled or not.

Selecting this option will apply the chosen pattern to the four corner locations out of the nine total locations which exist at level 1 (and 4×8 corner locations out of 9×8 total locations at level 2, etc.). The four locations positioned halfway between two corners will be drawn with a simple block filling.
Selecting this option will apply the chosen pattern to the four locations which are positioned halfway between two corner locations, that is four locations out of the nine total locations which exist at level 1 (and 4×8 mid-point locations out of 9×8 total locations at level 2, etc.). The four corner locations will be drawn with a simple block filling.
When this option is selected the chosen pattern will be applied as the filling in every possible location. From the nine locations that exist in each grid, no location will consistently have a simple rectangular block filling. However, the filling for the centre of the whole image (level 0) can be controlled separately using the "Patterned centre" tick box. The pattern depth control also has an impact.
This controls how many levels of detail have the chosen pattern applied to them. If there are more levels of detail drawn in total than the number of levels with a pattern then the finest levels of detail will be drawn using a simple rectangular block filling. The total number of levels of detail can be changed using a control located under the Geometry tab.
This is roughly equal to the maximum number of rectangles (or rather rectangle approximations) contained within a collection of concentric rectangle approximations in a location filled with the pattern.
This makes the coloured bands thicker and the transparent bands simultaneously thinner, or the coloured bands thinner and the transparent bands thicker.
This controls how far the top-left corner of a coloured band sticks out towards the centre of the shape.
This controls the angle of the top edge of each band.
This controls the position of the bottom-left corner of each band as well as the angles of the left edge and the bottom edge. Move the slider further to the left to push the corner outward or move the slider to the right to move the corner closer to the centre.
This controls the thickness of the bottom part of each coloured band.
Bipartite colouring means organization into two different kinds of pieces with two different colours. When this option is ticked the centre locations (1 at level 1, 8×1 at level 2, etc.) will be coloured using two different colours (black and blue by default).
This controls how many levels of detail are drawn opaquely without any translucency effect applied to the filling. The opacity slider for the central portion will be ignored initially and translucency will only be applied at the finer levels of detail. This option will have no effect if the centre opacity slider is positioned fully to the right, which is the default position.
This controls how many times rectangles are painted with colour. No background painting is performed at level 1. When set to 2 then the 8 large outer rectangles have their backgrounds painted once but when finer details are added only the centre rectangles are painted. When set to 3 then 8 large rectangles are painted followed by 8×8 smaller rectangles also painted with colour and everywhere except the very centre ends up being painted twice. You can build up layered effects using opacities and blend modes.
This is number of levels of detail to draw. At level 1 only a single rectangle is drawn. At level 2 the centre rectangle is surrounded by 8 smaller rectangles. At level 3 each those 8 rectangles is surrounded by 8 further rectangles, and so on.
Moving this slider to the right makes the left column bigger and right column smaller. Conversely, moving the slider towards the left makes the left column narrower and the right column wider. To make both columns wider or both columns narrower use the Stretch slider instead.
Moving this slider to the right makes the top row bigger and bottom row smaller. Conversely, moving the slider to the left makes the top row shorter and the bottom row taller.
This slider makes the middle column wider or narrower.
